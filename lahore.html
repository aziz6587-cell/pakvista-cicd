🏛️ Lahore

City of Gardens — where Mughal grandeur, living heritage, and the spirit of Punjab come together in one extraordinary city.

About Lahore

Lahore, the capital of Punjab, is Pakistan's cultural heart and second-largest city. Founded over a thousand years ago, it served as the capital of the Mughal Empire during its golden age and carries the architectural legacy of emperors Akbar, Jahangir, Shah Jahan, and Aurangzeb. Today it is a thriving metropolis renowned for its food, music, literature, and some of the finest Islamic and Sikh-era monuments on the Indian subcontinent.

Best time to visit: October to March, when the weather is mild and ideal for exploring the walled city on foot. Annual highlights include the Lahore Literary Festival (February) and the Basant kite festival in spring.

Badshahi Mosque

Mughal Heritage

Commissioned by Emperor Aurangzeb in 1671 and completed in 1673, the Badshahi Mosque is one of the largest mosques in the world and the finest example of Mughal religious architecture. Its imposing red sandstone façade, four 62-metre minarets, and a central marble dome create a silhouette that has defined Lahore's skyline for over three centuries.

The mosque's courtyard — one of the largest in the world — is paved with brick laid in geometric patterns and can hold up to 100,000 worshippers during Friday prayers and Eid congregations. The main prayer hall is decorated with delicate white marble relief work, medallions of pietra dura, and frescoed pendentives that rival the craftsmanship found in the Taj Mahal.

Located directly opposite the Lahore Fort across Hazuri Bagh garden, the mosque forms part of a magnificent ensemble of Mughal monuments that together constitute a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Entry is free for worshippers; visitors are asked to remove footwear and dress modestly.

Lahore Fort (Shahi Qila)

UNESCO World Heritage

Lahore Fort, known locally as Shahi Qila ("Royal Fort"), is a walled palace complex spread over 20 hectares at the north-western edge of the old walled city. Its origins date to the 11th century, but the complex seen today was largely shaped by the Mughal emperors, particularly Akbar, Jahangir, and Shah Jahan, between the 16th and 17th centuries.

Within the fort's walls stand twenty-one notable monuments, including the Sheesh Mahal (Palace of Mirrors), whose ceiling and walls are encrusted with thousands of tiny convex glass pieces that scatter light in intricate patterns; the Naulakha Pavilion, a white marble pavilion decorated with pietra dura inlay work comparable to any surface in the Taj Mahal complex; and the Diwan-e-Aam (Hall of Public Audience), where the emperor received petitions from his subjects.

The fort is inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List alongside the Badshahi Mosque. A museum inside displays Mughal armour, miniature paintings, and artefacts spanning several centuries of the city's history.

Shalimar Gardens

UNESCO World Heritage

Built by Emperor Shah Jahan between 1641 and 1642 CE, the Shalimar Gardens represent the apogee of Mughal garden design — the charbagh ("four-garden") tradition that divides the garden into quadrants by water channels representing the four rivers of paradise described in the Quran.

The gardens are arranged across three descending terraces, each enclosed by high red-brick walls. The uppermost terrace, reserved historically for the emperor and his court, contains the most elaborate pavilions and the famous Aiwan-e-Khas, a marble hall where Shah Jahan would rest during visits. A central spine of water channels connects all three terraces, passing through a series of cascades, basins, and over 400 fountains whose simultaneous operation creates a curtain of water that fills the garden with the sound of flowing streams.

Listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1981, the gardens are a tranquil retreat from the city's bustle and are most beautiful in the cool months of October to February when flowers are in bloom. Evening illumination events are held seasonally.
